Linear Regression Hypothesis Testing Brand & Valuation

Does a bigger following mean a bigger valuation?

32 NFL teams · 2024 · franchise value vs. Facebook & Twitter following

Situation

Testing a common marketing assumption: does audience size predict value?

Using 2024 data on all 32 NFL teams, I tested whether franchise value could be explained by social media following — comparing Facebook fan counts and Twitter follower counts as two separate predictors.

Complication

Both were statistically significant. Neither explained most of the variance.

Both models passed their hypothesis tests (Facebook: p = 0.0003; Twitter: p = 0.007) and their residual diagnostics. But Facebook following explained only 35.4% of the variance in franchise value, and Twitter following explained just 21.5%. Statistical significance confirmed a real relationship — it didn't confirm a strong one.

# model: franchise value ~ facebook fans FV = 4.439 + 0.448(fb_fans) p-value: 0.0003 R²: 0.354 # model: franchise value ~ twitter followers FV = 4.376 + 0.618(tw_followers) p-value: 0.007 R²: 0.215
Resolution

I picked the stronger model — then stress-tested it on a real team.

Facebook following was the better predictor and the model I'd recommend between the two. To check whether "better" meant "reliable," I used the model to predict the Kansas City Chiefs' franchise value from their actual Facebook following, then compared it to their real 2024 valuation.

So what

The model predicted a franchise value of $5.74B for the Chiefs — their real value was $4.85B, a miss of roughly $890M. Reporting that gap matters more than the R² alone: a marketing or valuation team relying on this model would need other variables alongside social following, not in place of it. A model being "significant" doesn't mean it's "sufficient."
