When does a city actually need more bikes?

Seoul Bike Sharing System (UCI ML Repository) · summer season · hourly rentals

Situation

Forecasting hourly bike-share demand from weather and time data.

Using Seoul's public bike-share dataset, restricted to the summer season (its highest-demand period), I modeled the number of bikes rented per hour using eight predictor variables: hour of day, temperature, humidity, wind speed, visibility, dew point, solar radiation, and rainfall.

Complication

No single model is automatically the right one — so I compared three.

After transforming the response variable (a square-root transformation produced the most normal distribution of the options tested), I built and tuned three tree-based ensemble models using 5-fold cross-validation: a bagged tree, a random forest, and a boosted tree.

# test-set mean squared error by model Bagged Tree: 20.230 (n_estimators=100) Random Forest: 22.906 (n_estimators=1000) Boosted Tree: 26.729 (n_estimators=1000, lr=0.1) > most important predictor: Hour
Resolution

The bagged tree generalized best — and the demand pattern was unambiguous.

The bagged tree model had the lowest test-set error of the three and tracked actual rental counts most closely, especially around peak hours. Across all three models, hour of day was consistently the strongest predictor. A simplified decision tree made the pattern explicit: rentals spike around 8 AM and 6 PM, sunlight increases midday demand, and rainfall sharply suppresses rentals regardless of time of day.

So what

For a bike-share operator, this points to a concrete fleet-rebalancing rule: pre-position more bikes ahead of the two daily commute peaks, and scale back redistribution effort proactively on days with meaningful forecasted rainfall — rather than reacting to demand after it's already shifted.
