What separates a playoff team from the rest?

32 NFL teams · 2024 regular season · offensive & defensive team statistics

Situation

Coaches don't need a 6-dimensional model. They need to know what to practice.

Using 2024 regular-season stats for all 32 NFL teams — field goals made, passing and rushing touchdowns, interceptions, forced fumbles, and sacks — I built classification models to predict which teams made the playoffs. The goal wasn't just accuracy: it was to turn the result into something a coaching staff could actually use, specifically for the Kansas City Chiefs.

Complication

A model that uses all six stats at once is accurate, but unreadable to a coach.

I tested support vector machine classifiers across three kernels — linear, polynomial, and radial basis function (RBF) — tuned with 5-fold cross-validation. The RBF kernel had zero misclassifications on the training set. But a 6-dimensional decision boundary isn't something an offensive or defensive coordinator can visualize or act on. The technically best model was the least useful one to hand to a coach.

# SVM kernel comparison (5-fold CV) linear (C=0.1): 1 misclassified polynomial (C=0.1, d=2): 1 misclassified RBF (C=10, gamma=0.01): 0 misclassified
Resolution

I broke the model into two-variable views a coordinator could actually read.

Instead of presenting the full 6-variable model, I built six separate two-variable linear-kernel comparisons — one set for offensive stats (paired combinations of field goals, passing TDs, rushing TDs), one for defensive stats (forced fumbles, interceptions, sacks) — and plotted the Chiefs against the rest of the league on each.

The pattern held across views: passing and rushing touchdowns separated playoff teams from non-playoff teams far better than field goals did, and on defense, interceptions and sacks were stronger separators than forced fumbles.

So what

The Chiefs ranked below most playoff teams on rushing touchdowns, passing touchdowns, and interceptions in this dataset — giving the coaching staff three specific, data-backed areas to prioritize in practice, instead of a vague "improve overall offense" recommendation.
