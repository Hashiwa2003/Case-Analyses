Regression Model Diagnostics Pricing

A good R² isn't permission to deploy.

Event ticket sales dataset · 76 transactions · stepwise variable selection

Situation

Which factors actually drive what a ticket sells for?

Working from a ticket sales dataset, I set out to explain ticket price using five candidate variables: seat row number, whether the seat was midfield, whether it was on the aisle, how many days before the event the ticket was purchased, and which day of the week the game fell on.

Complication

The best-fitting model looked strong — until I checked its assumptions.

Using forward stepwise selection and 4-fold cross-validation, the model with three predictors — row number, location, and aisle seating — came out on top, explaining 80.4% of the variance in ticket price with the lowest cross-validation error of any model tested.

# model comparison by predictor count 1 predictor: adj. R² .713 2 predictors: adj. R² .794 3 predictors: adj. R² .804 (lowest CV error) # diagnostic check Shapiro-Wilk on residuals: p = 0.003 > residuals not normally distributed

But the diagnostic checks told a different story: the residuals were bimodal, not normally distributed, and a Shapiro-Wilk test confirmed it (p = 0.003). A model can explain 80% of the variance and still produce p-values you shouldn't trust.

Resolution

I recommended against deploying the model as-is.

Despite having the strongest fit and lowest error of any model tested, I flagged this model as not ready for use in pricing decisions, and recommended a data transformation (such as a Box-Cox transformation) as the next step before the model could be trusted for real recommendations.

So what

It's tempting to ship the model with the best R². The harder, more valuable skill is checking whether a model's own assumptions hold before recommending anyone act on it — and being willing to say "not yet" when they don't.
